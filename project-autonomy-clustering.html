Autonomy-Aware Clustering: When Local Decisions Supersede Global Prescriptions

Status: Under Review at ICLR 2026
Role: Lead Researcher
Year: 2024-2025
Reinforcement Learning Clustering Optimization Stochastic Systems Machine Learning

Overview

This project introduces a novel approach to clustering that accounts for local stochastic autonomy in complex systems. Traditional clustering algorithms assume that agents will perfectly follow prescribed cluster assignments. However, in real-world scenarios, agents often have their own autonomous decision-making processes that may deviate from global prescriptions.

Our reinforcement learning-based framework learns to make clustering decisions that are robust to this autonomy, achieving over 10× improvement compared to traditional autonomy-agnostic baselines.

Key Features

  • Reinforcement Learning Framework: Novel RL approach that learns optimal clustering under local autonomy constraints
  • Stochastic Autonomy Modeling: Probabilistic model of agent behavior that captures deviation from prescribed actions
  • Scalable Algorithm: Efficient computation that scales to large numbers of agents and clusters
  • Performance Guarantees: Theoretical bounds on clustering quality under autonomy
  • Real-world Validation: Tested on multiple application domains including robotics and network systems

Technical Approach

The core innovation lies in formulating the clustering problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) where:

  • States represent current cluster configurations and agent autonomy patterns
  • Actions correspond to cluster assignment decisions
  • Rewards are based on clustering quality metrics that account for autonomy-induced deviations
  • Policy learning uses deep Q-networks with custom reward shaping

The algorithm iteratively refines clustering decisions by observing how agents respond to assignments and adapting the clustering strategy to minimize the impact of autonomous deviations.

Results

Experimental evaluation on benchmark datasets demonstrated:

  • 10× improvement in clustering quality compared to k-means and spectral clustering
  • 15× improvement over hierarchical clustering methods
  • Robust performance across varying levels of agent autonomy
  • Computational efficiency comparable to traditional methods
  • Generalization to unseen autonomy patterns
